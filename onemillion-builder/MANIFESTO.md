# The Age of Agentic Engineering

*By Sid Dixit*

---

There has never been a stranger time to know how to build software.

For thirty years the answer to "how do I build an app?" was: learn to code, study for years, get a degree if you can afford one, apprentice at a company, then maybe — *maybe* — you'll be trusted to ship something to real users. The path was long, expensive, gated, and mostly closed to people who weren't 22 years old with a Computer Science degree and a $400,000 line of credit from their parents.

That path is dying. Not slowly. Not gradually. **Right now, in front of us, it is dying.**

I'm not saying this to be provocative. I'm saying it because I watched it happen.

In 2023, I needed to ship a side project. I had the idea, the spec, the customer interviews. I didn't have time for the six months it would have taken me to build it the old way. So I tried something new: I gave the spec to Claude and asked it to write the code. Then I asked it to fix the bugs. Then I asked it to deploy. Three days later — three days, not three months — there was a working product on the internet, with real users on it.

Something in me broke loose that week. The thing I had assumed was hard had become easy. The thing that took six months took three days. And the thing that made the difference wasn't a new programming language or a new framework. It was a new *relationship* with the machine.

I had stopped being someone who writes code. I had become someone who **directs intelligence**.

This is what I want to teach you. Not how to code. Not how to use a particular AI tool. How to **direct intelligence** — how to think clearly about what you want, decompose it into the right shapes, hand pieces to the right agents, verify what they produce, and ship the result. The skill is not in the typing. The skill is in the thinking. And every person reading this — yes, you — has that skill latent in them already.

## The world is splitting

Look around your office, your industry, your LinkedIn feed. You will see two kinds of people forming.

The first kind treats AI like a chatbot. They use it to write a quick email, summarize a document, brainstorm a list. The AI is a slightly faster version of Google for them. They get small wins. They feel productive. They are not thinking about what's coming.

The second kind treats AI as a builder. They give it specifications. They ask it to write code, ship features, deploy systems. They review what it produces. They guide it. They iterate. They ship real software, real products, real businesses — at a pace that ten years ago would have required teams of engineers and quarters of budget. They are not faster than the first group. They are operating in a different dimension.

In ten years, there will be no third option. You'll either be the first kind or the second kind. The wages, the careers, the opportunities — they will split along this line. The split has already started; you can see it in the indie hackers shipping SaaS solo, in the PMs prototyping products without engineering teams, in the consultants billing for agentic workflows their clients didn't know they needed.

OneMillion exists for one reason: **to make sure as many people as possible end up on the right side of that split.**

Not the ones who can afford bootcamps. Not the ones with CS degrees. Not the ones with technical co-founders. Everyone. Yoga teachers. Nurses. Retirees. Executive assistants. Career changers. Anyone with an idea and the will to build it should not be locked out because of money or background or the year they were born.

## The Five Pillars

If you teach someone to use ChatGPT, you've given them a tool. If you teach them to **build with AI**, you've given them a craft.

These are the five pillars of agentic engineering. Every product built well is built with these. Every product built badly skips one or more. The course threads them through every day of the 18 days, until they become how you think.

**Spec before code.** Never let AI generate without a written spec it can validate against. Vague prompts produce vague results. The art is in writing the spec well enough that the AI can build it without needing to guess.

**Multi-agent decomposition.** One big agent doing one big task fails. Many small agents with clear roles succeed. The skill is in breaking the work into the right shapes and handing each piece to the right specialist.

**Validation gates.** Every phase has acceptance criteria. The AI checks itself against them. If it passes, advance. If it fails, iterate. This is how you go from "the AI said it worked" to "the work actually works."

**Production hygiene from day 1.** Auth, security, monitoring — these are foundation, not afterthought. If you build a product that's insecure, you don't have a product. You have a liability.

**The human review loop.** AI proposes. You dispose. You spot-check, not because the AI is dumb, but because you're the one who's accountable. The skill is knowing where to look — what to trust, what to question, what to override.

These five things, internalized, are the difference between a vibe-coder who built a demo and an engineer who built a business. The course is the path. The pillars are the destination.

## Free, forever, on purpose

The hardest thing about this work is not the writing. The hardest thing is the principle: **everything must remain free, forever, for everyone.**

Free, because the people who most need this are the people who can least afford to pay. A $20,000 bootcamp doesn't reach the assistant in Mumbai who has an idea for a small business. A $200 Udemy course doesn't reach the retiree in Detroit who wants to build something for his community. A $50/month subscription doesn't reach the student in Lagos who has more talent than capital.

The skill of building with AI cannot become a class signal. It cannot be a gated credential. It must be a baseline — available to anyone, anywhere, with a laptop and the will to learn.

That's why OneMillion is on GitHub, MIT licensed, with no upsells, no premium tier, no certificate fee. The cohorts are free. The verification is free. The Builder number is free. Forever.

I'm not doing this for charity. I'm doing it because I believe — *I know* — that the world is better when more people can build. Every problem worth solving has been waiting for someone to build the solution. Every idea worth shipping has been sitting in someone's head for years because they couldn't afford to learn how. Every community that wanted a tool of its own had to wait for an outsider to build it, and usually that outsider built the wrong thing.

We're going to fix that. One million people. One million products. One million proofs that the skill is available to anyone, that the gatekeepers were lying about how hard this was, that the divide is not technical but motivational.

That's the gift. That's why this exists. That's why the million starts with one.

You.
