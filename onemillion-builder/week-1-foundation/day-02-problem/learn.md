## Part 1: Why Everyone Lies To You (~10 min read)

You're going to share your idea with friends and family today. Maybe a co-worker. Maybe your spouse.

Almost all of them will say "oh wow, that's such a great idea, I'd totally use that."

They are lying. **Not maliciously.** They love you. They want to be supportive. They don't want to hurt your feelings or make you feel stupid for being excited. So they say nice things.

Then you build the product, you ship it, you show it to them, they nod and say "cool!" — and they never use it. Because they were never going to use it. They were just being kind.

This is the trap. It has killed more startups than competitors, bad timing, and bad design combined. It is the single biggest reason that people who try to build products fail. They built something nobody wanted, because the people they asked told them they wanted it.

The fix is a book. It's called **The Mom Test**, by Rob Fitzpatrick. It's short. You don't need to read it (we'll give you the essentials below) but if you do read it, your skill at building things gets permanently upgraded.

The premise: **don't ask people about your idea. Ask them about their life.**

## Part 2: Bad Questions vs Good Questions (~10 min read)

Bad questions trigger lies. Good questions surface truth.

### Bad questions (don't use these)

| Question | Why it's bad |
|----------|--------------|
| "Would you use this?" | Hypothetical. People always say yes to hypotheticals. |
| "Do you like this idea?" | Asks for opinion. People say nice things to be supportive. |
| "If this existed, how much would you pay?" | Future commitment. People over-promise on imagined products. |
| "Could you see yourself using it weekly?" | Imagined future use. Same problem as above. |
| "What features would you want?" | Asks them to design your product. They're not the builder; you are. |

### Good questions (use these instead)

| Question | What it surfaces |
|----------|------------------|
| "Walk me through the last time this problem happened." | Specific past behavior — not aspirational future behavior |
| "What did you try to solve it?" | Whether they took action — pain real enough to act on |
| "What would it cost you not to solve this?" | Quantifies the pain — time, money, relationships |
| "When was the last time it happened? What were you doing?" | Anchors them to a specific moment, not a generic complaint |
| "Tell me about the workaround you have today." | Reveals existing solutions — competition for your product |

**The principle:** ask about past behavior, not future intent. People misrepresent the future. They can't misrepresent what they actually did last Tuesday.

> 💡 **Specific stories beat general statements.** "Scheduling is hard" is useless. "Last Sunday I spent 90 minutes manually checking which clients I owed deliverables to" is gold. Push for the specific.

## Part 3: The Five-Conversation Rule (~10 min read)

Before your PRD is locked on Day 3, you need **five real conversations**. Not surveys. Not "I asked my Slack channel and got 20 replies." Conversations — phone, video, in-person, voice messages — where you ask good questions and listen.

**Why five?** Three is too few — could be a coincidence. Five is enough to spot patterns. Beyond five gives diminishing returns until you've shipped something. Five.

**Who counts?**
- Anyone with the problem you're trying to solve
- NOT your spouse, parents, best friend (Mom Test rule)
- NOT random LinkedIn connections who don't have the problem
- ✅ Strangers in relevant forums (Reddit, Discord) you DM with care
- ✅ Friends-of-friends who match your target user
- ✅ Coworkers if your idea is about a work problem
- ✅ Yourself (kind of — see below)

**What if you ARE the user?**
If your idea solves a problem you personally have, count yourself as ONE conversation. Write up what you've actually done, when, and why. Then go find four other humans with the same pain. You cannot interview only yourself.

**What if the conversations kill your idea?**
Then your idea was bad and you just saved yourself 18 days. Pick a different problem and start over. Many builders pivot between Day 1 and Day 3. That's not failure — that's the system working.

## Two Paths — Pick Yours

We learned in simulations that the original Day 2 had too much friction for non-networked builders. So we offer two paths. Both count.

### Full Path (RECOMMENDED if you can)
**3 conversations with target users you don't know well.**
Cold DMs to strangers. Friends-of-friends. Reddit posters. People who clearly have the problem.

This produces the strongest evidence and the deepest insight.

### Light Path (OK if you can't do Full)
**2 internal conversations + yourself = 3.**

"Internal" means: people you already know who have the problem. A coworker. A friend who fits the target. A family member if they genuinely have the problem (NOT your mom unless she's actually a freelance designer or whatever you're building for).

"Yourself" means: you write up your own experience of the problem — when did it happen, what did you try, what did it cost you. You count as ONE conversation if you have the problem firsthand.

**If you take Light Path:** add a stretch goal — do 3 cold conversations in **Week 2** as homework. The validation gets deeper. But don't let Day 2 block you.

## Today's Assignment

Have **3 conversations today** (Full OR Light path — your call).

For each conversation, write a record in `.onemillion/notes.md` with:

```
## Conversation 1: [Name], [Role/Context]
**Date:** 2026-05-19
**How:** Phone / Zoom / Slack / In person

### The pain story they told (specific):
[What did they describe? Walk through their actual experience.]

### What they've tried to solve it:
[Existing tools, workarounds, hacks, abandoned attempts]

### Cost of not solving (their words):
[Time spent, money spent, frustration, relationships affected]

### Direct quotes:
- "..."
- "..."

### Vibes — does this person really feel this pain?
[1-3 sentences. Trust your gut here.]
```

Three of these. Real people. Today.

## What Good Looks Like

A great Mom Test conversation:
- Lasted 15-30 minutes
- Had the user describe a specific moment in detail (when, where, with whom, what they did next)
- Surfaced 1-2 things they tried that didn't work
- Made you go "oh damn, I didn't realize that"
- Left you with at least one direct quote you'd use in marketing

**Real example (from the bootcamp):**

> Sarah, 34, freelance UX designer:
>
> "Last Sunday, I spent an hour and a half trying to figure out which of my four clients I still owed mockups to. I had to cross-reference Notion (where I track projects), my email (where most discussion happens), and Slack (where some clients DM me) — and I still wasn't sure I'd remembered everything. I lost a client last year because I forgot to send them the final files for two weeks. They went with someone else."
>
> **Direct quote:** "I'd pay $50/month to never feel that Sunday panic again."
>
> **Note:** Pay attention to the $50/month. She *volunteered* a price. That means the pain is real enough she's already thought about what it would be worth to solve.

That's a great conversation. Three of those and you have a real product on your hands.

## Common Mistakes (Today)

1. **Pitching your idea.** Conversations should be 80% them talking, 20% you. If you find yourself describing your idea for more than 2 minutes, you've already lost the data.

2. **Talking to wrong people.** Your spouse is supportive. Your friend is kind. Neither has the problem. Find people who have the problem.

3. **Cherry-picking.** Only counting conversations that validated your idea, ignoring ones that didn't. The skeptics teach you more than the enthusiasts.

4. **Surveys instead of conversations.** Surveys give you data shaped like answers people think they should give. Conversations give you the messy truth.

5. **Stopping after one "yes."** One person with the problem doesn't make a product. Five doesn't either, but it's a signal.
